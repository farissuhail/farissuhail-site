The scholars

Four traditions of scholarship study this structure

Do not take our word for it — read what each tradition actually argues.

Ḥamīd al-Dīn al-Farāhī & Amīn Aḥsan Iṣlāḥī

The modern coherence (naẓm) tradition: every sūrah has a central pillar-theme (ʿamūd), and sūrahs come in pairs.

Michel Cuypers

Semitic Rhetorical Analysis: parallelism, mirror (ABC‖C′B′A′), and concentric (ABC-X-C′B′A′) figures at every scale. Book-length studies of Q5 (The Banquet) and Q81–114 (A Qur'anic Apocalypse).

Raymond Farrin

Structure and Qur'anic Interpretation (2014): ring analyses of Q1, Q2 and Q3, pairing across the whole Qur'an, and the claim that the entire book forms one grand concentric design.

Neal Robinson, Angelika Neuwirth & Nevin Reda

Structural analyses of long sūrahs and Meccan sūrah form that ring theorists build on.

Read this honestly
Before you explore

Ring composition in the Qur'an is a live scholarly debate, not a settled fact. Nicolai Sinai's 2017 review essay argues that many of Cuypers' and Farrin's schemes rely on flexible section boundaries and selective emphasis — a careful reader "will have reason to doubt" a number of them.

This atlas therefore diagrams only structures with a published basis, cites the analyst for each, and marks every unmapped sūrah as unmapped rather than inventing rings for all 114. Where you see a diagram, treat it as one scholar's argument about the text — a lens, not a decree.
