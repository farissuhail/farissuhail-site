Thinking · Psychology

Thinking,
Fast and Slow

Daniel Kahneman
★★★★★Live2011

What it's about. A Nobel laureate maps the mind as two systems: System 1, the fast and automatic one that runs on gut feeling, and System 2, the slow and deliberate one we'd rather not wake. Most of our errors come from letting the fast system answer questions it isn't qualified for.

Ideas that stayed with me
  • Two systems — fast intuition vs. slow reasoning — quietly trade control.
  • Anchoring: the first number you hear warps every estimate after it.
  • “What you see is all there is” — we judge on the little we know and ignore the rest.
  • Losing hurts about twice as much as winning the same amount feels good.

Who it's for. Anyone who makes decisions under uncertainty — investors, leaders, and honestly all of us.

One idea to keep: when a judgment feels effortless and certain, that's exactly the moment to slow down and check it.

Investing · Risk

The Most
Important Thing

Howard Marks
★★★★★Live2011

What it's about. Distilled from decades of investor memos, Marks argues that successful investing isn't one thing but many — price versus value, real risk, cycles, and above all the discipline to think one level deeper than the crowd.

Ideas that stayed with me
  • Second-level thinking: don't ask “is it good?” — ask “is it better than the price assumes?”
  • Real risk is the chance of permanent loss, not how much the price wobbles.
  • Markets move in cycles; knowing where you stand matters more than predicting.
  • “If we avoid the losers, the winners take care of themselves.”

Who it's for. Investors ready to move past tips and slogans into how risk and psychology actually work.

One idea to keep: protect the downside first — survival is what lets you compound.

Investing · Value

Margin
of Safety

Seth A. Klarman
★★★★½Live1991

What it's about. A famously out-of-print classic on risk-averse value investing. Klarman's whole stance is humble: you will be wrong sometimes, so buy assets far enough below their worth that being wrong still leaves you safe.

Ideas that stayed with me
  • A margin of safety is the gap between price and value that absorbs your mistakes.
  • Beating the market while losing money is still losing — chase absolute returns, not relative.
  • The herd manufactures overpriced, overcrowded assets; patience is the edge.
  • The investor's first job is simply not to lose money.

Who it's for. Patient, long-term investors who value capital preservation over excitement.

One idea to keep: never confuse a rising price with a sound decision; demand a discount before you act.

Investing · Stock-picking

One Up On
Wall Street

Peter Lynch
★★★★½Live1989

What it's about. A legendary fund manager makes a cheerful case that ordinary people have real edges over Wall Street — you notice good products and businesses in daily life long before the analysts do, if you do the homework.

Ideas that stayed with me
  • Invest in what you understand — then verify it with real research.
  • Sort companies into types — each behaves differently.
  • The two-minute drill: if you can't explain the thesis simply, you don't have one.
  • Individual investors aren't forced to trade on anyone's schedule — that freedom is an edge.

Who it's for. Beginner-to-intermediate stock pickers who want a practical, encouraging starting point.

One idea to keep: know the story of every holding well enough to retell it in two minutes — or don't own it.

Thinking · Probability

Fooled by
Randomness

Nassim N. Taleb
★★★★½Live2001

What it's about. Taleb on the hidden role of chance — how easily we mistake luck for skill, especially in markets, and why the smart move is to judge the process and survive the rare disaster rather than admire the outcome.

Ideas that stayed with me
  • A good outcome can still come from a reckless bet — judge the decision, not the result.
  • Survivorship bias: we only see the winners, so success looks easier than it is.
  • Hindsight makes the past feel inevitable when it never was.
  • Above all: survive the rare catastrophe — the black swan you didn't price in.

Who it's for. Anyone who judges people and decisions purely by results — and wants to stop.

One idea to keep: a wise plan can still meet a bad year; build so that one bad year can't end you.
